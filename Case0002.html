The Number That Knows
When to Say "I Do"

Inside an 80-year-old puzzle about hiring secretaries lies a formula for the most human decision of all — and it hinges on a constant that shows up everywhere in nature.
Scroll to begin

Johannes Kepler had a problem. It was 1611, his first wife had died of cholera, and the great astronomer — the man who had decoded the orbits of planets — found himself staring at a far more bewildering mystery: how to choose a second wife.

He did what any self-respecting scientist would do. He made a list. Eleven women. He visited them, one by one, over the course of two painful years. The first candidate was lovely but had bad breath. The second seemed interested only in his modest fortune. Number three was engaged to someone else. By the fifth candidate Kepler felt a spark, but she seemed too young, so he kept going. Numbers six through ten all disappointed. Desperate, he circled back to candidate five — who, mercifully, was still available — and married her. It was, by his own reckoning, the happiest decision of his life.

Four centuries later, mathematicians would prove that Kepler's instinct was strikingly close to optimal. Not emotionally optimal, not spiritually optimal, but mathematically optimal — in a sense so precise that it involves a number etched into the fabric of the universe itself.

That number is e. Or, more specifically, 1/e. Which works out to about 0.368. Which means roughly 37%.

And it answers the question that every person who has ever dated, swiped, wondered, or agonized has asked themselves at two in the morning: How do you know when to stop looking?

The Cruelest Game Show in the World

The Problem

Here's the setup, stripped of all sentimentality. Imagine you'll meet exactly N potential partners over the course of your romantic life. They show up one at a time, in random order. After each date, you get a clear sense of how this person compares to everyone you've already met — but you have no way of knowing what's still coming. And crucially, once you pass on someone, they're gone. No callbacks. No "let me think about it." You say yes or you say goodbye forever.

If this sounds cruel, that's because it is. But it's also recognisably life. We don't get to line up all our options side by side like paint swatches. We meet people in sequence, and we're perpetually guessing whether the person in front of us is the one who got away or just the one who showed up early.

Mathematicians call this the Secretary Problem — originally framed as a manager interviewing applicants for a single position, forced to hire or reject each one on the spot. It goes by other names too: the Marriage Problem, the Sultan's Dowry Problem, or, in the driest corners of the literature, simply optimal stopping. The framing changes but the mathematics does not. Whether you're hiring a secretary, choosing a spouse, or deciding when to sell a stock, the underlying question is identical: when you see options one at a time, with no going back, how do you know when to stop?

The question, then, is tactical: is there a strategy — any strategy at all — that gives you the best possible chance of ending up with the single best person in the whole pool?

Remarkably, there is. And before I show you the math, I'd like you to feel it in your bones. Try the game below. You'll meet candidates one by one, see how they compare, and have to decide — right then, no mulling it over — whether to commit. Play it a few times. Notice what happens when you jump too soon, and what happens when you wait too long.

Why Your Gut Instinct Doesn't Work

Naive Strategies

Before we reach for calculus, let's acknowledge what most people actually try — and why it fails.

The romantic: "I'll know it when I feel it." This translates to picking the first person who dazzles you. With a pool of 100, that gives you a 1% chance of success. Your heart may be pure, but your odds are abysmal.

The perfectionist: "I'll wait until I've seen them all." But by then, every single candidate has walked out the door. You end up with whoever happens to be last — which has exactly the same 1% chance of being the best. Waiting for everything is mathematically identical to choosing nothing.

The pragmatist: "I'll take the current best whenever they show up." Sounds smart, but watch what happens. If the second person you meet is slightly above average, you commit immediately — with 98% of the pool still unseen. This greedy strategy is like declaring a restaurant your favourite after visiting exactly two.

What all these strategies share is a failure to separate learning from choosing. They conflate two fundamentally different activities: gathering intelligence about the world, and acting on that intelligence. The math tells us these are not the same thing, and the boundary between them has a precise address.

Where Calculus Meets Commitment

The Proof

Here is the idea, stripped to its skeleton. You decide in advance to reject the first r candidates, no matter what. You're not wasting this time — you're calibrating. You're learning what "good" looks like so that when "great" walks through the door, you recognise it. After those r observations, you commit to the very next person who is better than everyone you've seen.

The question becomes purely mathematical: what value of r gives you the highest probability of landing the best candidate overall?

Counting the ways to win

Suppose the best candidate sits at position k in the queue. You win if two things happen simultaneously. First, the best person must arrive after your exploration window — otherwise you're forced to reject them. Second, nobody between position r + 1 and k − 1 can be good enough to trigger your acceptance — which means the best of the first k − 1 people must fall within your exploration zone.

The probability of that second condition is simply r / (k − 1). Sum this over every possible position of the best candidate, and you get:

P(r, N) = Σ from k = r+1 to N  of  (1/N) · r/(k−1)

= (r / N) · Σ from j = r to N−1  of  (1 / j)

The continuous trick

Now for the move that turns a sum into a symphony. For large N, let x = r/N — the fraction of candidates you explore. The sum of 1/j becomes an integral, and the whole expression collapses to something clean and beautiful:

P(x) ≈ x · ∫ from x to 1 of (1/t) dt

= x · (−ln x)

= −x · ln(x)

Finding the peak

Differentiate. Set to zero. And out tumbles one of the most satisfying results in all of probability theory:

dP/dx = −ln(x) − 1 = 0

ln(x) = −1   →   x = e−1 = 1/e ≈ 0.3679

Maximum success probability:   P(1/e) = 1/e ≈ 36.8%

Read that again. The optimal fraction to explore is 1/e. And the maximum probability of success — even with the best possible strategy — is also 1/e. The same transcendental constant appears in the question and the answer. This is not a coincidence. It falls directly from the structure of the equation, and it means that e is simultaneously telling you how long to wait and how likely you are to win if you do.

The universe hid the answer to "when should I commit?" inside the same constant that governs compound interest, radioactive decay, and the spiral of a nautilus shell.

A Problem in Search of an Author

History

Like many great ideas, the secretary problem has origins that are slightly disreputable and wonderfully unclear. It almost certainly circulated as a parlour puzzle in the 1950s — the kind of problem mathematicians scribble on napkins. The earliest known formal statement appeared in a RAND Corporation paper around 1960, but nobody is quite sure who wrote it down first.

The Cast of Characters

Martin Gardner (1914–2010)

The problem's journey into popular culture belongs almost entirely to Martin Gardner, whose February 1960 "Mathematical Games" column in Scientific American recast it as the "Sultan's Dowry Problem" — a sultan choosing the most beautiful of N candidates in sequence. Gardner understood, perhaps better than anyone alive, that the best mathematics travels inside a story that makes people feel something.

The definitive proof came in a 1966 paper by John P. Gilbert and Frederick Mosteller in the Journal of the American Statistical Association. And then there is the Kepler footnote — our astronomer, methodically evaluating eleven candidates, circling back to number five, and writing afterwards with obvious satisfaction that careful comparison had led him to the best choice. Whether he solved an optimal stopping problem or was simply being Kepler, we cannot say.

The Explore–Exploit Dilemma

The Deeper Pattern

The secretary problem is a single movement in a much larger symphony. The tension it captures — gather information or act on it? — surfaces everywhere.

A bumblebee deciding when to leave one flower patch for the next is solving a cousin of this problem, tuned by millions of years of natural selection. A venture capitalist screening startups, an employer interviewing job applicants, a web browser deciding whether to keep loading or use cached data — they are all navigating the same fork in the road. The mathematics changes in the details, but the structure is the same: there comes a moment when one more observation costs more than it teaches, and at that moment, you must act.

The 37% rule is not just a number. It's a proof that patience and decisiveness are not opposites. Each has its place, and the dividing line between them is a transcendental number.

"The optimal amount of time to spend gathering data before acting is never zero, and never everything — it's always somewhere in between. In this case, it's precisely 1/e." — Brian Christian & Tom Griffiths, Algorithms to Live By

The Asterisk (Because Life Is Not a Math Problem)

Caveats

Of course, life isn't this clean. The classical model assumes you know how many candidates you'll ever meet (you don't). It assumes preferences are stable (they shift). It assumes rejected candidates can never return (sometimes they can, as Kepler proved). And it assumes there is a single objectively best person for you — a claim that is, to put it gently, philosophically fraught.

But that's not the lesson. The lesson is that even the most profoundly human question — when do I stop looking and commit? — contains within it something precise enough to be solved. The answer won't tell you whom to love. But it will tell you something important about how to search: that the reckless and the timid both lose, that learning requires a period of deliberate non-commitment, and that the turning point between exploration and action has a name.

Its name is 1/e.

Next time someone asks, "How did you know they were the one?" you'll have an answer that is simultaneously romantic and mathematical: I watched, I learned, and after about 37%, I trusted what I'd seen.

It's not a guarantee. It's just the best anyone can do under uncertainty. And maybe that's enough.
